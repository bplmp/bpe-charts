  • These are our best estimates, based on the most recent available data; quantities are approximate. If you have data that differs, please send us an email with that data and the recommended update.
  • Viable end market options for gypsum were in part informed by USA Gypsum (https://www.usagypsum.com/drywall-recycling) and the NEWMOA (http://www.newmoa.org/solidwaste/GypsumWallboardRecyclingWhitePaperFinal9-17-10.pdf) Whitepaper: "Promoting Greater Recycling of Gypsum Wallboard from Construction and Demolition (C&D) Projects in the Northeast."
  • (1) Includes wallboard from renovations. CDRA, 2017: "Benefits of Construction and Demolition Debris Recycling in the US." Considering 73% C&D wallboard going to landfill (excluding trim scraps), same report.
  • (2) Considering 2019 US production of wallboard of 24,000 million square feet (USGS, 2020); wallboard density of 0.00105 tons per square foot for a 5/8" thickness board (City of Napa). This would mean 25.2 million metric tons of new wallboard produced per year, 10% of that being scraps.
  • (3) Considering gypsum in C&D fines to be proportional to quantity of gypsum drywall in all C&D, and considering that all gypum fines are going to landfill. CDRA, 2017. In a potential scenario, we could greatly reduce fines (considering 90% reduction) in C&D by source-separating Gypsum Wallboard.
  • (4) Considering a 50% recycling rate for trim scraps, and a 50/50 split in recycled trim scraps between new wallboard and soil amendment. Total quantity of new wallboard recycled from USGS, 2020.
  • (5) Considering that up to 15% of new wallboard could be made of post-consumer material. Total quantity of new wallboard from USGS, 2020.
  • (6) Today, the maximum percentage of post-consumer wallboard in cement is 1%, but we're considering that this figure could go up to 5%, which is the maximum percentage for gypsum in new cement. Total quantity of new wallboard from USGS, 2020.
  • (7) Considering 3.49 million tons of gypsum used in soil amendment. CDRA, 2017.
  • (8) Considering that 10% of wallboard used in jobsites becomes trim scraps.
